Conditions

What are conditions?

Conditions in D&D are basically just debuffs in any other turn based rpg. Each condition is different in its own way. If you fail the saving throw in your attempt to avoid the condition you will be debuffed. These things can make or break a fight. You being there at the wrong place at the wrong time can end you in a snap. Now that that's out and about let's go over each condition.

Blindness

Blindness is of course a debuff that makes you blind. You can't see, well anything, for a period of time. No enemies around you, allies, nothing. For the mechanics of the game the affected creature will have disadvantage on its attack rolls as well as those who attack the affected creature get advantage. Not only that, but the affected creature will automatically fail any ability checks pertaining to sight.

Charmed

Being charmed is like being coerced into thinking that your greatest enemy is your bestest friend. Not only will you not harbor any malicous feelings towards them, but they get advantage on ability checks that involve socializing with you. If you fail the save you can no longer attack the caster. However, you will get a chance to remake the save everytime you take damage.

Deafened

Just like blindness, deafened is pretty straight forward. You can't hear a thing if you fail the saving throw. The affected creature, just like blindness, will automatically fail any ability check that involves hearing. Pretty straight forward.

Frightened

Frightened is another one of those pretty obvious conditions. If you fail the save you will be scared of the caster. Terrified and afraid you have little choice but to run. The effected creature has disadvantage on all ability and attack rolls while the source of their fear is still in sight. Additionally, they can't move any closer to the source of their fear either.

Grappled

The grappled condition occurs when an enemy attempts to grab you. If you fail the save you are grappled and held in place by the enemy. While grappled you can't move. You'll have to spend your turn trying to get the grappler off of you or trying to kill them. You can also cast spells or use aspects of your class to try and stun them or knock them back.

Incapacitated

Incapacitated doesn't necesarily mean unconscious or pinned down. There are several spells in this game that fuck with a creatures mind. Incapacitated just means that a creature loses the will to or is forced to stop fighting. An incapacitated creature cannot make actions or reactions. Hence they cannot do anything but move around the battle field.

Invisible

Now this is one of the conditions that isn't a negative one. Think of it more as a buff than a debuff. An invisible creature cannot be seen, unless magic or special senses are utilized. Afterall, they are invisible. Enemies who try to attack an affected creature do so at disadvantage.

Paralyzed

Paralyzed creatures are well, paralyzed. They can't move or speak and they even follow the same rules as incapacitated creatures. Moreover, a paralyzed creature automatically fails a Str or Dex saving throw. So if a Str or Dex spell hits you, you will take full damage no matter what. Not only that, but enemies who attack the effected creature get advantage on their attack rolls and will automatically crit if they are within 5ft. of the effected creature.

Petrified

The petrified condition pertains to being transformed and imobilized in some sort of way, usually by stone. The affected creature also gains weight by a factor of 10 and is unaware of its surroundings. It follows the incapacitated condition, and, just like paralyzation, the affected creature automatically fails Str and Dex saves, can't move, can't talk, and enemies who attack the affected creature attacks at advantage. However, the affected creature also gains all resistances. Resistances are things that can help you halve specific damage. For example, if my character has a resistance to fire damage, all fire damage done to me is halved. There are also immunities and vulnerabilities that your character can have. If your character has an immunity to a specific damage type then you will never take any damage from that specific type. Meanwhile, if your character has a vulnerability to a damage type, you will take double the amount of damage from that type. You can aquire these from what race you pick, certain feats, and what class you choose.

Poisoned

A poisoned creature is exactly how it sounds. If you fail the save, you will take poison damage over time until you die or finaly make the save. Furthermore, the affected creature will also recieve disadvantage on attack roles and ability checks since you are basically dying of poison.

Prone

The prone condition occurs when an enemy knocks you on your ass. You will be prone until you stand up, which you can only do on your turn. If a party member helps you up during their turn it will also end the condition. When prone, enemies get advantage on their attack rolls if they're within 5 feet of you. If they aren't they get disadvantage. While you are on the ground you will also have disadvantage on attack rolls until you get up.

Restrained

A restrained creature is one who is tied up or tangled in something. It's basically when a character gets a bit too intertwined in an oppsticale. Now, an affected creature cannot move at all and has disadvantage on their attack rolls. Meanwhile, opponents get advantage when attacking the affected creature.

Stunned

The stunned condition is what you expect. It's basically that old cartoon you watched as a kid where the bad guy gets smaked and gets little starts floating around his head. The affected creature is again incapacitated. They can't move, however they can speak just falteringly. The creature will automatically fail Dex and Str saving throws and again people who attack the affected creature will get advantage.

Unconscious

Unconsciousness can happen in 2 different ways. Either 1) a spell knocks you unconscious, say sleep for example, or 2) you drop to or below 0 health. Unconsciousness is one of the most dangerous conditions you can have, because it usually means you are about to die. Now since there are 2 ways to go unconscious there are 2 ways it can affect you. First let's start with the effects that happen in both ways. When you are unconscious you are incapacitated, automatically fail Str and Dex saving throws, are unaware of your surroundings, can't move, can't speak, falls prone, enemies get advantage against affected creature, and will automatically crit if they are 5 feet from them. Now if you are knocked unconscious by a spell this is all that pretains to you. However, if you are knocked unconscious because you are at or below 0 health than you have to do something extra. This something extra is called a death save. It is where you roll a straight d20 to see if you can get back up. 1-10 you fail 11-20 you succeed. Now you need to fail 3 times in order to die and the same goes for if you want to get back up. Additionally, if you get attacked while you are unconscious you will recieve an automatic failure, but if you are healed while unconscious you will get back up with how ever many hit points you were healed for.

Exhaustion

Exhaustion is a condition that you can really only cause yourself. This condition only happens if you don't sleep. There are a total of 6 levels of exhaustion. Each day you don't sleep each level you go up. See the table below to see the debuffs you can get from exhaustion.

Exhaustion Level Debuff Recieved
1 Disadvantage on all Ability Checks
2 Speed Halved
3 Disadvantage on Attack Rolls and Saving Throws
4 HP Max Halved
5 Character Can no Longer Move
6 Death
